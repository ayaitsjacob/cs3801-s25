Surveillance and Civil Liberties

Surveillance technology has advanced significantly in the past decade due to improvements in artificial intelligence, data storage, and network connectivity. Governments and private companies now use tools like GPS tracking, facial recognition, and internet activity monitoring on a wide scale. These tools can be beneficial—helping law enforcement solve crimes, enhancing public safety, and optimizing urban planning. However, they also raise serious concerns about civil liberties, particularly the right to privacy and freedom of expression. When people know they’re being watched, they may self-censor or feel unsafe. This tension creates a critical ethical dilemma: how do we ensure public safety without compromising the basic rights of individuals?

The Utilitarian Perspective

Utilitarianism is an ethical theory developed by philosophers like Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill. It promotes the idea that actions are morally right if they maximize happiness and reduce suffering for the greatest number of people. Applied to surveillance, a utilitarian might argue that if monitoring technologies prevent crimes, reduce terrorism, and improve safety for millions, then the benefits justify the privacy costs. However, this view becomes problematic if surveillance causes widespread anxiety, leads to discrimination, or gives authorities too much power with too little accountability. The key question becomes: are we actually creating more good than harm? Utilitarianism requires us to weigh both visible benefits (like reduced crime) and hidden harms (like psychological stress or loss of freedom).

Real-World Examples

China

In China, surveillance is deeply integrated into daily life. The government uses millions of cameras equipped with facial recognition to monitor citizens’ movements, sometimes even inside apartment buildings. Online activity is tracked and censored, and people’s behaviors can affect their social credit scores, which determine access to travel, loans, and jobs. While Chinese authorities argue that such systems help maintain order and reduce crime, critics say they suppress free speech, discourage political dissent, and invade personal privacy on a massive scale. From a utilitarian standpoint, if the negative impact—millions living in fear or self-censorship—outweighs the number of crimes prevented, then the surveillance is ethically unjustified.

London

London is often cited as one of the most heavily surveilled cities in the Western world, with over 600,000 CCTV cameras in use. These cameras are employed to deter crime, monitor traffic, and provide evidence during investigations. However, unlike in some authoritarian regimes, the UK has laws and oversight bodies that regulate surveillance, such as the Information Commissioner’s Office. Public consultations and legal checks help ensure that surveillance tools are not abused. This example represents a more balanced approach: one that attempts to protect public safety while still respecting individual rights. From a utilitarian view, London’s model may better align with ethical surveillance because it includes accountability and aims to minimize harm.

Practical Takeaways

For surveillance to be ethical and publicly acceptable, it must operate under clear and transparent rules. Citizens should have access to information about what data is collected, who collects it, how it's used, and how long it is stored. Government agencies should be required to obtain judicial warrants before accessing sensitive data like personal messages or location histories. It’s also crucial to ensure that surveillance doesn’t unfairly target marginalized communities, which can lead to systemic discrimination. Independent watchdogs, privacy commissions, and input from civil society can all help keep surveillance in check and promote trust between governments and citizens.

What You Can Do

As individuals, we’re not powerless. You can start by asking key questions: Is my phone tracking me? Who has access to my data? What rights do I have if I want it deleted? Supporting organizations like the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) or the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) helps promote privacy rights and challenge unjust surveillance laws. You can also take personal steps: limit permissions on your apps, use encrypted messaging services, and stay informed about local privacy legislation. Finally, advocate for laws that increase transparency and protect personal data in your community.
