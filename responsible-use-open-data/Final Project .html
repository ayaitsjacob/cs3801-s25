Overview

Open data is a free dataset that anyone can access, use, or edit as long as there is internet access. Examples of open data would be weather data, demographic information, and the political and economic state of the world. The main goal of open data is to improve transparency, innovation, and public engagement. Government and organizations around the world have published countless datasets to the public, with the very hope that people can use them to inform other people, build useful tools, and understand social and economic trends. Well, despite these benefits, ethical concerns are on the rise. Since there have been incidents of privacy violations, misuse of data, lack of credit to the researcher, misinformation, or unfair access to technology to get hold of open data. While the intention behind open data is very noble, the risks of using it shouldn’t be ignored. Some responsible usage should mean ensuring accuracy, preventing harm from happening, protecting individual privacy, and ensuring that the data being published to the public doesn’t have anything malicious or misleading. This is all very important as data has a significant impact on people’s lives; it is essential to develop and follow guidelines that promote its ethical use and access to it.

Kantian Ethics

An ethical framework we could look through would be Kantian ethics, which is grounded in universal moral laws and treating individuals as ends in themselves-not merely as means to an end. This perspective shows that the act of sharing data should come from a sense of moral duty. Open data, as a whole, aligns with this duty because it supports education and innovation. Regardless, Kantian ethics also emphasizes the importance of consent and respect. Publishing data without any sort of permission goes against this principle. Moreover, using a researcher's data without credit or any sort of compensation, regardless of whether it is freely available, could still be seen as disrespecting their work.

Utilitarianism

Another ethical framework that can also be used for this topic is utilitarianism, since it focuses on he greatest good for the greatest number. Open data can lead to a lot of positive outcomes, like increasing public knowledge, better decision-making, and social benefits. Again, we can’t ignore any of the cons, like how some data can be used to reinforce stereotypes or discrimination, and then the overall utility is compromised. Utilitarianism challenges data user to carefully consider both the benefits and potential harm of their action.

Examples

A controversial example is the case of Aaron Swartz, a digital rights activist who downloaded millions of academic articles from JSTOR via MIT’s network, intending to make them freely available. MIT and the federal prosecutor accused him of fraud. Swarts argued that he acted on moral grounds, promoting public access to knowledge. This case shows the ethical tension. Swarts sought to serve the public good, however, he went through the legal and institutional norms. Through the lens of both Kantian and Utilitarianism ethics, the situation is very complex as his motives were grounded in him to benefit others, though the outcome was his persecution and even suicide. This highlights the danger of unbalanced approaches to access and accountability.

Practical takeaways

  • -Is there a license indicating permitted use?
  • -Are the original creators or data sources given credit?
  • -Could the data harm a certain group of people?
  • -Are the limits of the data clearly explained?
  • - What information is personal or private?
  • - Is this data accessible and usable by everyone?
  • - Who benefits most from this data being open?
