Ethical Design for Mental Health & Wellbeing

Alexander Ibanez
Georgina Mateo

OVERVIEW

Within the past few years, the developments in social media have given rise to multiple phenomena negatively affecting the mental health and well-being of its users, namely doomscrolling and brainrot.

DOOMSCROLLING

Doomscrolling is a behavioral issue where users spend an excessive amount of time browsing social media, driven by platform features designed to keep them engaged. Persuasive and manipulative algorithms on these platforms encourage prolonged use, often keeping users on the app for numerous hours at a time, often to unhealthy degrees.

In addition to a decrease in productivity and wasting time, doomscrolling has also been associated with clinical issues such as anxiety, depression, and insomnia. According to an article on UC San Diego Today, the human body “may respond to repeated bad news as if it were in continuous danger, involving changes like stress hormone surges, increased heart rate and feeling on edge or exhausted.” When exposed to this type of negative social media for long periods of time, it could contribute to anxiety or depression.

BRAINROT

On the other hand, “brainrot” refers to low quality internet content and the negative psychological effects that arise as a result of consumption. While oftentimes, brainrot is viewed as the cultural slang developed by younger generations, the brainrot being studied here refers to the apparent negative effects on users’ attention span.

One article on BMC Public Health studied the correlation between the consumption of short form video content and psychological disorders in college students. Specifically they found, “a higher propensity for short video addiction may impair executive control,” and "a strong negative correlation between short video addiction and self-control abilities." It then went on to explain how prolonged exposure engages lower-order cortical brain regions while suppressing the higher order areas that are used for self control and attention among other things.

MITIGATION

The following are features that developers could implement.

PARENTAL CONTROL SETTINGS

Parental controls help limit the time users spend on social media. This allows parents to set personalized screen time rules and reduce the potential of their child becoming addicted to these services, ultimately leading to the encouragement of building healthier online habits from a young age.

TRANSPARENCY IN DESIGN

Users will have full transparency of how applications work and how they may affect them after prolonged periods. This information will highlight the importance of monitoring users on their screen time.

MODERATED EXPLORE PAGE

A fairly moderated explore page will avoid presenting users with masses of argumentative or highly stimulating content. By promoting variety and positive content, platforms will encourage healthier and mindful browsing habits among users.

NATURAL BREAKS

Messages such as “You’re all caught up” give users a clear point on when to stop scrolling. Without these messages, people may scroll without realizing how long they have been on the app or understand how much content they’ve consumed

DISPLAY OF SESSION TIME

A timer helps raise user awareness of screen time, allowing users to organize their time on social media more effectively.

RED FLAG NOTIFICATIONS

Platforms should monitor when users post or engage with depressive or harmful content and provide gentle check-ins or notifications suggesting healthier alternatives or support. This can include mental health resources, support hotlines, or reminders to take a break. Other users should also be able to report concerning behavior, helping to trigger these supportive messages. These features aim to protect users’ mental health and create a safer online environment.

ETHICAL FRAMEWORK

The following is a guiding ethical approach that developers can use when creating mental health and wellbeing tools.

VIRTUE ETHICS

Virtue ethics focuses on being a good person and doing what’s right. For developers, this means designing mental health tools with kindness, honesty, and care. Instead of just following rules or aiming for profits, designers guided by virtue ethics think about how their work affects others. They aim to support users by creating apps that are honest, helpful, and respectful, especially when dealing with sensitive topics like anxiety, depression, and addiction.

MILL

Mill’s utilitarianism focuses on doing what brings the greatest happiness to the greatest number of people. For mental health design, this means weighing the benefits of features like mood tracking or reminders against any harm they might cause, such as anxiety or over-reliance. Ethical tools should increase overall wellbeing while minimizing possible negative effects.

KANT

Kant’s Categorical Imperative teaches that we should only act in ways we’d want everyone else to act, and that we must always treat people as ends in themselves, never just as a means to an end. In the context of mental health technology, this means developers have a duty to respect users’ autonomy, privacy, and dignity. For example, collecting sensitive mental health data without clear consent, or designing features that exploit user behavior for profit, would violate this principle. An ethically designed mental health app, guided by the Categorical Imperative, would instead be transparent, fair, and supportive—something we’d want all mental health tools to be if the practice became universal.

RAWLS

Rawls argued that a just society is one that protects the rights of the least advantaged. In digital mental health tools, this means ensuring that privacy is not a luxury, but a right for everyone. Users entrusting an app with sensitive information—like anxiety levels, depression symptoms, or therapy history—deserve systems that guard that data rigorously. According to Rawls, ethical design would create platforms where mental health support is equally accessible and where no one is put at risk simply for seeking help. Developers must design with fairness, equity, and user safety in mind.
